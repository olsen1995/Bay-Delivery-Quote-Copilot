# Bay Delivery Market Research Report for North Bay, Ontario

Archive note: This research document was imported from a ChatGPT research session and normalized for repository use. Source links have been converted to standard Markdown where available. Any unresolved items are labelled for verification.

## Executive findings

The published local hauling market in North Bay is wide enough that Bay Delivery should not chase the cheapest operator. At the informal end, Man & A 1/2 Ton [source verification needed] advertises jobs “starting at $80 per load.” At the professional local end, [705 Junk](https://705junk.ca/faq) posts $100 minimum, $194 for a quarter load, $299 for a half load, and $499 for a full load, while [Mr. Junk](https://mr-junk.ca/pricing/) posts a $75 minimum and rates up to $700 for a full load. Bay Delivery’s best positioning is therefore not “cheapest in town.” It is “professional owner-operator, slightly under bigger polished operators when access is easy, but clearly above casual cash-hauler pricing.”

The hardest local cost anchor is disposal, not advertising. The [City of North Bay landfill schedule](https://northbay.ca/services-payments/garbage-recycling/landfill-operations/tipping-fees/) shows $10 for six bags or less, $25 for seven or more, $35 for a double load, $118/tonne for weighed dual-axle residential loads, and $118/tonne for industrial/commercial/institutional waste, rising to $236/tonne when mixed with recyclables or banned material. Merrick also charges $30 per mattress/box spring and $25 per refrigerant appliance. Those municipal fees are low enough that Bay Delivery can still make money on normal jobs, but high enough that dense debris, mattresses, refrigerant appliances, and mixed loads should never be treated as “just another dump run.”

Small moving jobs are the most likely place Bay Delivery could undercharge. In posted Ontario moving rates, Ottawa Pro Movers [source verification needed] shows 2 movers + truck at $129 to $139 per hour with a 3-hour minimum, and 3 movers + truck at $175 per hour. Move It Right [source verification needed] shows furniture moving starting at $129 with hourly, door-to-door pricing and a 2-hour minimum. In Toronto, Toronto GTA Movers [source verification needed] shows how quickly a “small” move becomes expensive: 2 movers at $55 per mover per hour, 3-hour minimum, plus a $165 truck fee equals $495 before tax. The Canadian benchmark from movers.ca [source verification needed] is $120 to $200 per hour for a 2-person crew with a truck. Bay Delivery should treat moving as labour-plus-vehicle work, not as delivery work with a few extra minutes.

Demolition and rip-out work is even more sensitive. The City’s demolition checklist [source verification needed] shows a $109 permit fee, contractor liability insurance of at least $2 million, a consent-to-guarantee-tipping-fees form, and additional reports when required. Public North Bay demo pricing is weak, but Ontario benchmarks are not: small shed demolition commonly falls around $300 to $600 for small sheds and $600 to $1,000 for medium ones, bathroom strip-outs are often quoted from roughly $2,200 to $5,500, kitchen guts from roughly $3,500 to $8,500, and flooring removal commonly runs by the square foot. Bay Delivery should auto-price only light, clearly described, non-structural rip-outs. Most room demos, shed demos, dense debris loads, and anything suspicious should be quote-only.

## North Bay disposal rules and hard cost floors

The local disposal environment is the clearest hard-cost input Bay Delivery has. Merrick Landfill is approved for designated waste from the City of North Bay, the Municipality of Callander, and certain unorganized townships, and the site accepts debit, Visa, and Mastercard, not cash. Bay Delivery should therefore treat waste-classification risk and payment-flow risk as operational realities, especially on surrounding-area, business, trailer, and demo jobs.

The arithmetic behind the risk is simple. At the posted $118/tonne rate, a half-tonne disposal-heavy load is about $59 in tipping alone; if the load is classed as mixed, the same half-tonne is about $118. A full one-tonne mixed load is $236 before fuel, labour, truck wear, and profit. Bay Delivery’s quote system should therefore force a different pricing path for concrete, brick, tile, shingles, plaster, wet drywall, dirt, and mixed construction debris. That is a direct inference from the City’s fee table, and it is the safer pricing assumption.

Demolition creates another local floor. The City’s demolition checklist shows a $109 local permit fee, a consent-to-guarantee-tipping-fees form, a site plan, proof of at least $2 million in liability insurance, and designated-substance reporting where required. Even if Bay Delivery is only doing strip-out work and cleanup rather than full structural demolition, room demos should be treated as materially different from junk removal.

## Small moving market deep dive

North Bay’s established movers reinforce the same conclusion by omission: ABLE Moving Services [source verification needed] and Van’s Delivery, Moving and Storage [source verification needed] both prefer quotes over public rate cards, and Van’s specifically asks for an in-person walkthrough and deposit. Surrounding-area moves are also quote-based in local delivery: [Martin Services](https://martinservices.org/) explicitly says deliveries beyond its core service area are available by quote. Bay Delivery should therefore auto-price only tightly scoped in-town small moves; surrounding-area moves should either carry zone pricing or move to manual review.

### Profitable-but-underpriced moving jobs

The best margin opportunities are often the jobs customers mentally classify as “too small to call movers.” That includes a couch from Facebook Marketplace, a washer and dryer relocate, a mattress and bed frame move, a small apartment downsizing job, or a one-bedroom move where the client has already packed. The market says those jobs still justify professional minimums: one-piece delivery can start around $120 to $180, furniture movers start around $129 with minimum hours, and real 2-mover local jobs frequently carry 2- to 3-hour minimum blocks. Bay Delivery is likely underpriced if it quotes these as flat “pickup and drop-off” jobs without labour logic.

### Low-margin moving jobs

The worst margin shows up when access, waiting, and scope uncertainty eat the clock. Apartment walk-ups, unreserved condo elevators, multi-stop Marketplace routes, partial move-outs where the client is not packed, oversized sectionals, appliance pairs, long hallway carries, narrow staircases, and surrounding-area moves can turn a small invoice into a long day. The posted Ontario market consistently charges for that friction, either through minimum hours, door-to-door time billing, or explicit surcharges for stairs, access, manpower, or assembly. Bay Delivery should not auto-price these at one-item convenience rates.

Hazard and suspicious-material warnings are non-negotiable. Merrick separately prices asbestos and contaminated fill, requires pre-approval for both, and bans liquids, pathological waste, electronics as landfill, and other special materials. North Bay’s demolition checklist can require a designated-substance report. The 2026 calendar also says the HHW depot does not accept unidentified items, explosives, biological/pathological waste, radioactive waste, or refrigerant items. Bay Delivery should never auto-price unknown drums, unknown powders, fuel/oil containers, suspect asbestos, mold-heavy demolition, chemical storage cleanouts, or anything with an obvious environmental or building-code issue.

| Construction/debris cleanup, disposal-heavy | $250+ minimum before heavy-material adjustments | $283+ | Weight and disposal risk make low minimums dangerous. |

### Services most likely underpriced today

Small moving jobs are the first place to audit. If Bay Delivery currently prices couch moves, appliance moves, one-bedroom moves, or apartment jobs like simple delivery work, those jobs are probably underpriced relative to the Ontario market. The published pro market charges real crew minimums, and even one-piece work often lands around $120 to $180 once weight, floor level, or assembly is involved.

Demolition debris cleanup is the second major underpricing risk. Light demo, carpet/floor rip-out, and construction debris may look like “junk,” but municipal weight-based tipping, heavy-material surcharges, and higher labour needs make those jobs more expensive to perform than ordinary household loads. If Bay Delivery does not already separate normal junk from reno/demo debris in its quote logic, it is almost certainly leaving margin on the table.

Mattress jobs, refrigerant appliances, and “free scrap” calls also deserve a pricing audit. Mattresses cost $30 each at Merrick, refrigerant appliances cost $25 each, and North Bay’s free scrap/drop-off systems still leave Bay’s labour unpaid. These categories should never be priced with a generic “small load” shortcut.

### Higher minimums, helper triggers, and access triggers

Bay Delivery should set a materially higher minimum on any job that requires a helper, stairs, awkward carry, internal placement, or demolition labour. Ontario market references repeatedly show that easy curbside jobs get discounts while harder-access jobs carry add-ons. A practical version for Bay is: helper required = move into a 2-person labour path; stairs beyond one easy flight = surcharge or manual review; long carry, elevator delay, door removal, or on-site disassembly = surcharge or time-based pricing.

Recommended helper triggers are straightforward. One helper should be mandatory or strongly defaulted for fridges, freezers, washer/dryer pairs, sectionals, large dressers, packed apartment moves, and most in-home moves involving appliances or multiple bulky items. Two workers should also be the default for most light demo labour that includes tear-out and haul-out, not just a pickup of staged debris. Whirlpool’s extra-manpower pricing and Ontario mover crew ladders both support this.

### Demolition-specific quote-system rules

| Rule | Recommendation | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum demolition price floor | Do not auto-quote any demo/rip-out task below a $175–$250 cash minimum | Demo setup, PPE, cleaner load-out, and disposal risk are materially higher than on basic junk jobs. |
| Half-day labour logic | Offer labour-only demo as hourly or half-day blocks, not as “small-job” flat pricing | Ontario labour and hybrid models point that way. |
| Flooring rip-out logic | Use square footage + material type + haul-away/disposal, not one flat price | Carpet, vinyl/laminate, tile, and hardwood behave very differently. |
| Heavy/dense debris trigger | Concrete, brick, tile, plaster, shingles, dirt, wet drywall, mixed reno debris = manual review or weight-based surcharge | Tipping can jump sharply and load size stops being a reliable proxy. |
| Awkward access trigger | Basement demo, multiple flights of stairs, condo corridors, long carry, tight exits, poor parking = higher minimum or review | Access directly slows labour and raises handling risk. |
| Disposal-risk trigger | Mattress count, refrigerant items, mixed loads, wood/brush seasonality, asbestos/contaminated fill = separate variables | Local disposal fees and restrictions are item-specific. |
| Quote-only trigger | Bathroom demos, kitchen demos, sheds, decks, utility-connected items, anything with cabinetry/tile/subfloor uncertainty | Ontario market treats these as quote-based because hidden conditions matter. |
| Never auto-price | Asbestos, contaminated fill, unidentified chemicals, fuel/oil containers, mold-heavy demo, structural demo, permit-dependent structure teardown | Regulatory, safety, and liability risk are too high. |

### Jobs that should usually stay automatic vs move to manual review

Automatic pricing works best on curbside household junk, pure scrap-metal curbside pickups, one-person simple deliveries, clearly staged small moves, and floor-rip jobs with known square footage and known material. Those jobs have enough repeatability to standardize with minimums and surcharges.

Manual review should take over on apartment moves with uncertain access, surrounding-area jobs, multi-stop routes, dense/mixed construction debris, demolition beyond minor cosmetic rip-out, shed demos, bathroom or kitchen demos, jobs containing appliances plus stairs, and any suspicious or potentially hazardous material. That is where published local and Ontario market behavior becomes quote-first for good reason.

### Limitations

North Bay public web pricing is strong for junk removal and landfill rules, but weak for established local movers and demolition contractors. In this research, the local movers and local demolition-style operators found publicly tended to use quote requests, site visits, or walkthroughs instead of posted hourly menus. That weakness is why this report uses North Bay data first, then Ontario posted-rate comparables, then Canada-wide benchmarks. Where Ontario or Canada-wide data is used to set Bay Delivery targets, those bands are explicitly recommendations rather than verified North Bay competitor prices.
