# North Bay Disposal Cost and Risk Matrix for a Local Hauling Quote System

Archive note: This research document was imported from a ChatGPT research session and normalized for repository use. Source links have been converted to standard Markdown where available. Any unresolved items are labelled for verification.

This report is scoped to North Bay, Ontario, the Merrick Landfill, the City’s Waste Diversion Facility at 112 Patton Street, and the local scrap route you said you use, AIM Recycling North Bay [source verification needed]. All fees below are in CAD. “Verified” means directly supported by an official source. “Assumption” means a placeholder only where public sources conflict or do not publish enough detail. “Recommendation” means an operating rule for the quote system. Merrick is approved to accept designated waste from North Bay, Callander, and certain unorganized townships, and the landfill accepts debit or credit only, not cash.

## Executive summary

The disposal side of your pricing should not be treated like one generic “dump run” line. North Bay’s current system mixes flat residential charges, per-piece charges, free-but-separated diversion streams, weighed trailer rates, mixed-load penalties, resident-oriented depot programs, and approval-only streams such as asbestos and contaminated soil. That means a simple volume-only or “one trailer = one price” model will underprice some of the ugliest jobs and overprice some of the easy ones.

The biggest margin-risk jobs are weighed dual-axle loads, mixed loads containing recyclable or banned material, dense loads like brick or concrete, demolition debris where weight and composition are unclear, refrigerant appliances, multiple mattress pieces, suspected bedbug or biohazard mattresses, contaminated soil, asbestos, and any job relying on resident-oriented free-drop routes such as HHW or electronics without confirmation that a contractor delivery is allowed. North Bay’s own materials show that mixed loads can be charged at double rates, mattresses and refrigerant appliances carry separate fees, and asbestos and contaminated soil require pre-approval and testing.

The best pricing structure for your business is a hybrid model: labour/access/trailer pricing separated from disposal pricing; flat verified landfill fees used only where the fee is clean and predictable; special-item line items for mattresses, box springs/foam tops, refrigerant appliances, and similar items; weight-band or manual-review pricing for dense and weighed loads; and no promised scrap credit until the actual yard accepts and prices the material. That recommendation follows directly from North Bay’s fee structure, the mixed-load rules, the resident-oriented wording for some diversion programs, and AIM’s own note that pricing and acceptance questions should be checked with the yard.

Because your own quote system treats tax separately, that missing detail matters. Until you confirm it with the landfill, do not bake in an assumption that landfill charges are either always tax-included or always tax-extra.

The practical pricing lesson is simple: **sorting saves money only when the route is truly available to you and the item is actually accepted there**. Cardboard, clean metal, electronics, tires, yard waste, and some fill materials can all avoid normal garbage fees when clean and separated, but once they are mixed into a garbage or demo load, you can lose that advantage fast. North Bay explicitly doubles residential mixed-load fees when recyclables or banned material are in the load, and its Waste Diversion Strategy says the mixed-load fee exists for unsorted separable materials such as wood.

A second important point: several free-diversion programs are clearly written for residents, not contractors. The Waste Diversion Facility description says it helps residents manage materials that do not belong in curbside collection, and the HHW page is explicitly limited to residents of North Bay and participating municipalities. That does **not** prove a contractor delivery will always be rejected, but it is enough uncertainty that your quote system should flag HHW, propane, and other resident-program material for confirmation before you promise a free route.

### Mattress, box-spring, and foam-top rules

North Bay’s current mattress fee is straightforward: $30.00 per piece, and the City’s mattress definition includes mattresses, box springs, and foam tops of all sizes. Merrick’s mattress recycling program sends material out for recycling rather than burying it, and the City says the broader program ships mattresses and box springs to Ontario Mattress Recycling in Barrie, where about 95% of each piece can be recycled.

The catch is contamination. Merrick says mattresses or box springs known or suspected to contain bedbugs or biohazardous substances such as blood, feces, or urine must be treated as waste and taken to the active landfilling area instead of the normal mattress holding area. That means “mattress count only” is not enough for automation; you also need a contamination question.

### Appliance and refrigerant-appliance rules

Large appliances cannot be left at the curb in North Bay. The City says broken appliances can be taken to Merrick based on the fee schedule or may be accepted by a local scrap dealer. Merrick charges $25.00 for appliances containing refrigerant, including refrigerators, freezers, air conditioners, dehumidifiers, and some water coolers. Those same refrigerant items are banned from the HHW depot, the electronics stream, and the scrap-metal bins at the Waste Diversion Facility.

On the provincial side, Ontario’s O. Reg. 463/10 prohibits discharging refrigerant into the environment or within a building, and it says no one may service or test refrigeration equipment containing refrigerant unless they are certified and have access to recovery equipment. That matters operationally: if a fridge is damaged, cut, or altered before proper handling, the cost exposure stops being a simple $25 appliance fee and becomes a serious liability problem. For your quote system, refrigerant appliances should therefore be a separate route code and a separate fee code, never folded into generic “appliance pickup.”

The most important construction-pricing truth is that **volume is not cost**. A trailer that looks “half full” of brick, wet wood, shingles, concrete, or subfloor can be a far nastier job than a full trailer of couches and bags. North Bay’s pricing proves that directly: clean concrete and brick can be free, yet they still create the highest vehicle, trailer, tire, brake, and loading-time risk. You should price those jobs primarily on density, access, and weight risk, not just visual volume.

That last row is where owner-operator businesses usually get blindsided. The disposal fee itself may be published, but the **job** is not predictable because approval requirements, rejections, sampling, customer misinformation, and route changes can all hit at once. Merrick requires contaminated soil lab work including PFAS non-detection and O. Reg. 558 result submission, and asbestos requires advance approval and notice. Those are not “click-to-quote” jobs.

### Truck and trailer disposal-risk model

Official manufacturer towing charts show that half-ton truck payload and max trailer ratings vary materially by cab, drivetrain, axle ratio, and transmission, and the charts explicitly say payload and max trailer weights are estimated values. They also recommend a weight-distributing hitch for trailers over 5,000 lb. Because the exact build of your 2015 truck was not specified, and even the 2019 Classic crew cab varies by configuration, the quote system should never assume either truck can safely do a “full dense load” just because the engine is a 5.7 HEMI. Use the actual door-sticker payload, hitch class, trailer empty weight, tire ratings, brake condition, and tongue-weight math before assigning dense jobs.

| Equipment choice | Best use | Do not use as default for | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Truck only | Very small junk runs, single appliances, small furniture, one-off pickups | Dense debris, brick, concrete, shingles, full demo cleanup | Use only when the job is clearly light and local. |
| Single-axle aluminum trailer | Light to medium dump runs, bulky furniture, bagged junk, light wood | Dense fill, full wet wood, bricks, concrete, roofing, unknown demo weight | Good default for normal household junk. |
| Double-axle aluminum trailer | Heavy scrap, dense wood, larger demo debris, fill materials, loads likely to be weighed | Blind auto-pricing, especially on unknown-density loads | Use as the heavy-material trailer, but pair it with manual review. |
| Older enclosed trailer | Dirty enclosed loads, mattresses, box springs, rough scrap, awkward light junk | Clean moving jobs, sharp demo debris that can damage interior badly | Best when containment matters more than appearance. |
| Newer enclosed trailer | Clean furniture/appliance delivery and weather-sensitive customer goods | Garbage, wet waste, mattresses, demolition debris, dirty scrap | Protect this trailer from dump work unless there is a clear reason not to. |

## Pricing model, manual-review boundaries, and example scenarios

### Recommended pricing model

I recommend this structure:

| Model | Where it works | Where it fails | Use recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Actual disposal cost + handling/admin margin | Flat-fee landfill jobs, single mattresses, refrigerant appliances, clear separated loads | Can still fail on weighed loads if weight is unknown | **Best default base model.** |
| Load-based tiers | Loose household junk and furniture where disposal follows the flat residential tiers | Dense fill, mixed C&D, scrap mixes, weighed trailers | **Use only for light, low-density work.** |
| Weight-band pricing | Brick, concrete, shingles, drywall, mixed demo, full scrap, dual-axle jobs | Customers often underestimate weight | **Use as estimate-range model, not fixed promise.** |
| Special-item line items | Mattresses, box springs, foam tops, refrigerant appliances, special tires count | Fails when contamination or routing changes | **Mandatory.** |
| Manual-review / quote-only | Asbestos, soil, HHW, commercial, mixed loads, unknown weight, basement cleanouts with special items | N/A | **Mandatory.** |

The backbone should be:

**Service charge** + **access/inside-removal charge** + **equipment/trailer charge** + **disposal line** + **special-item lines** + **customer-side HST if you are charging it under your current payment method rules**.

That structure protects margin because it stops disposal-heavy jobs from disappearing inside an hourly number. It also lets you preserve your current cash-versus-EMT quote logic without pretending that dump costs are stable when they are not. The verified North Bay fee system gives you enough reason to separate disposal clearly.

Two operational pricing rules matter most:

First, **never give a guaranteed scrap deduction before the yard accepts the material**. AIM’s North Bay page shows metals, batteries, and e-waste categories, and its Text & Earn page explicitly says customers should ask about pricing and whether an item is accepted. That means your system should treat scrap value as **post-acceptance upside**, not as part of the guaranteed base quote.

Second, **free landfill streams are not free jobs**. Clean concrete, brick, and some fill can be free at the landfill, but those are exactly the loads that hammer a half-ton truck and trailer package. Price dense work on risk, trips, and equipment exposure first, and treat disposal savings as a secondary factor.

### What should not be automated yet

These job types should stay out of instant firm-pricing for now:

- Heavy construction debris, especially brick, concrete, shingles, plaster, tile, or dense wood.
- Any dual-axle or likely weighed load.
- Mixed scrap + garbage.
- Mixed demo loads.
- Unknown dump weight.
- Basement cleanouts with appliances, mattresses, or wet/dirty debris.
- Refrigerant appliances with stairs, damage, or uncertain condition.
- Propane, HHW, liquids, leaking batteries, or other resident-program special waste.
- Asbestos, contaminated soil, or any “mystery material.”
- Commercial jobs with significant recyclables or out-of-catchment waste.
- Anything where the best disposal route depends on a live acceptance decision from the landfill or AIM.
